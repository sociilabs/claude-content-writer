# Anti-AI Writing Checklist

Run this audit after every draft. Based on Wikipedia's "Signs of AI writing" guide and refined through extensive editing practice.

The checklist works for any voice, any industry, any format. The goal: content that reads like a specific human wrote it, not like a language model generated it.

---

## Two-pass audit process

**Pass 1 — Identify.** Read the draft and ask: "What makes this obviously AI-generated?" List every remaining tell, no matter how minor.

**Pass 2 — Revise.** Eliminate every tell identified in Pass 1. Then read aloud — if any sentence sounds like it could appear in any article about any topic, it's too generic. Make it specific to this piece.

---

## Structural patterns to eliminate

### Parallel list structures
Four or more sentences with identical grammar. Humans don't catalogue points in perfectly symmetric sentences.

**AI pattern:** "IP terms that gave us nothing. Payment structures that shifted risk to us. Non-compete clauses that boxed us in. Liability terms that were one-sided."

**Human pattern:** Vary the structure. Combine some. Make one longer, one shorter. Let some be fragments. "The IP terms gave us nothing. Payment was structured to put all the risk on us, and the non-compete would've boxed us in for years."

### Rule of three
LLMs force ideas into groups of three. Three items in a series, three parallel clauses, three examples. Break the pattern — use two items, or four, or one elaborated point.

**AI pattern:** "streamlining processes, enhancing collaboration, and fostering alignment"

**Human pattern:** Pick the one that matters most and say it well. Or use two and let the sentence breathe.

### Triple anaphora
Three sentences starting with the same word or phrase.

**AI pattern:** "We can't afford to... We can't sign away... We can't agree to..."

**Human pattern:** Break the repetition. "We can't afford to work for months and fight over payment. Signing away IP wasn't an option. And those liability terms? We wouldn't put them in front of our own clients."

### Negative parallelisms
"It's not just X; it's Y" / "Not only... but also..."

**Fix:** Just state the point directly. These constructions are AI comfort food — they feel smart but add nothing.

### Throat-clearing openers
"Here's the thing," "Here's the reality," "The truth is," "Let me be clear," "In today's fast-paced world," "It's important to note that."

**Fix:** Delete them. Start with the actual point. If the sentence works without the opener, the opener was filler.

### Formulaic challenge-and-resolution
"Despite these challenges, [subject] continues to thrive." / "While [obstacle], [positive outcome]."

**Fix:** Be specific about what the challenge actually is and what specifically was done about it. Or cut the section if it's padding.

## Word-level patterns to eliminate

### High-frequency AI vocabulary
These words appear far more often in post-2023 AI text than in human writing. They're not banned — but if you find yourself reaching for them, pause and find a more specific word.

**Red-flag words:** Additionally, align with, crucial, delve, emphasizing, enduring, enhance, fostering, garner, highlight (as verb), interplay, intricate/intricacies, key (as adjective), landscape (abstract), pivotal, showcase, tapestry (abstract), testament, underscore (as verb), valuable, vibrant, leverage, synergy, groundbreaking, nestled, breathtaking, renowned, stunning, robust, seamless, cutting-edge, holistic.

### Filler phrases
Compress or delete:
- "In order to achieve this goal" → "To achieve this"
- "Due to the fact that" → "Because"
- "At this point in time" → "Now"
- "It is important to note that" → [delete entirely]
- "The system has the ability to" → "The system can"
- "In the event that" → "If"

### Copula avoidance
LLMs substitute elaborate constructions for simple "is/are/has."

**AI pattern:** "The platform serves as a testament to..." / "Gallery 825 stands as the exhibition space..."

**Human pattern:** "The platform works." / "Gallery 825 is the exhibition space."

### Excessive hedging
**AI pattern:** "It could potentially possibly be argued that the policy might have some effect."

**Human pattern:** "The policy may affect outcomes." Or just commit: "The policy affects outcomes."

## Style patterns to eliminate

### Em dash overuse
LLMs use em dashes (—) much more than humans. Use commas, periods, or parentheses instead in most cases. Allow occasional em dashes for genuine mid-sentence interjections, but cap at 2-3 per article.

### Bold overuse
Don't mechanically bold every key phrase. Reserve bold for framework labels, section callout headers, or genuinely critical terms. Not for emphasis in running prose.

### Inline-header vertical lists
Bullet lists where items start with bolded headers followed by colons.

**AI pattern:**
- **User Experience:** The user experience has been improved...
- **Performance:** Performance has been enhanced...
- **Security:** Security has been strengthened...

**Human pattern:** Convert to prose where possible: "The update improves the interface, speeds up load times through optimized algorithms, and adds end-to-end encryption." Use bullet lists only when the items are genuinely parallel and the list adds clarity.

### Generic positive conclusions
**AI pattern:** "The future looks bright. Exciting times lie ahead as we continue this journey toward excellence."

**Human pattern:** End with a concrete action, a specific next step, or a reframing of the stakes. Never end with inspirational vagueness.

### Emoji decoration
**AI pattern:** 🚀 **Launch Phase:** / 💡 **Key Insight:** / ✅ **Next Steps:**

**Human pattern:** Just write the content. Emojis in headers are an AI tell unless the user's brand voice specifically uses them.

## Rhythm and soul

Eliminating AI patterns is half the job. Sterile, voiceless writing is just as obvious as slop. Good writing has a human behind it.

### Signs of soulless writing (even if technically "clean"):
- Every sentence is the same length and structure
- No opinions, just neutral reporting
- No acknowledgment of uncertainty or mixed feelings
- No first-person perspective when appropriate
- No humor, edge, or personality
- Reads like a Wikipedia article or press release

### How to add voice:
- **Have opinions.** Don't just report facts — react to them. "I don't know how to feel about this" is more human than neutrally listing pros and cons.
- **Vary rhythm.** Short sentences. Then longer ones that take their time. Mix it up deliberately.
- **Be specific about feelings.** Not "this is concerning" but "there's something uncomfortable about watching a founder burn $100K on infrastructure they'll have to throw away."
- **Let some mess in.** Perfect structure feels algorithmic. An aside, a half-formed thought, a "but I digress" — these signal a human thinking in real time.
- **Use "I" when appropriate.** First person isn't unprofessional. "I keep coming back to..." or "Here's what gets me..." signals a real person.
- **Match the user's actual personality.** If they're witty, be witty. If they're blunt, be blunt. If they're warm, be warm. Don't default to "professional" — default to the specific human you're writing for.

## Final check questions

Before delivering any draft, ask yourself:

1. Could someone read this aloud at a conference and it would sound natural?
2. Are there more than 2-3 em dashes in the piece?
3. Are there any rule-of-three constructions I missed?
4. Are there parallel list structures with identical grammar?
5. Does the opening sound like a specific human's experience, or like "an AI was asked to write about this topic"?
6. Is the closing a concrete action or vague inspiration?
7. Are all case study details verifiable from what the user has provided?
8. If I removed the byline, would a reader think "this sounds like ChatGPT"?

If the answer to #8 is yes, revise until it's no.
